ICSE-FoSE 2026 · April 12–18 · Rio de Janeiro, Brazil

From Papers to Progress: Rethinking Knowledge Accumulation in Software Engineering

Jason Cusati and Chris Brown · Virginia Tech
Paper (ACM DL) Source Code Survey Data (Zenodo)

Abstract

Software engineering research has experienced rapid growth in both output and participation over the past decades. Yet concerns persist about the field's ability to accumulate, integrate, and reuse knowledge in ways that support long-term progress. To better understand how the community itself perceives these challenges, we analyze responses from the ICSE 2026 Future of Software Engineering pre-survey, which captures perspectives from 280 globally distributed and highly experienced researchers.

Our analysis reveals a tension between increasing research productivity and the limited mechanisms available for synthesizing results, tracking evolving claims, and supporting cumulative understanding over time. We diagnose four interrelated structural breakdowns and articulate four technology-agnostic principles for future research artifacts, positioning FOSE as a venue for experimenting with alternative artifact designs that support cumulative scientific progress.

Structural Breakdowns

If the community is experienced, productive, and global, yet still struggles to build on prior work, the barriers are structural:

  • Papers as Isolated Knowledge Units Claims get buried in prose; evidence linkages stay implicit; results freeze at publication time.
  • Loss of Context and Provenance Methodological rationale fades as work gets cited; carefully qualified findings become unqualified facts.
  • Claims Evolve Without Tracking Contradictions go unflagged; refinements stay implicit; claim relationships appear only in natural language.
  • Incentive Structures Favor Novelty Publication venues reward new techniques; replication and synthesis face higher acceptance bars.

Four Principles for Future Research Artifacts

1. Structured & Interpretable

Claims, evidence, and context should be explicit and directly accessible, not just embedded in prose.

2. Inspectable & Provenance-Aware

Artifacts should preserve the full provenance of claims, from raw data through decisions to results.

3. Long-Lived & Reusable

Artifacts should support evolution and reuse, not remain static snapshots frozen at publication.

4. Governed with Human Oversight

Community processes should ensure quality, integrity, and ethical responsibility for shared infrastructure.

Survey

We ground our diagnosis in the ICSE 2026 Future of Software Engineering pre-survey (n=280). Respondents span six geographic regions and represent a mature, productive community: 57% have 11+ years of SE research experience and 44% authored 11+ papers in the past three years. This profile matters because if knowledge accumulation remains limited despite all this expertise and output, the barriers are structural, not individual.

The survey data is publicly available on Zenodo.
